# Result 8 — paired GRU vs the classical-RL suite on the same 149 held-out mice

![paired GRU vs RL](../fig_rl_paired.png)

**What changed (2026-07-13).** This result originally compared the GRU against **one** classical model — Bari — and reported a +0.0136 margin. Issue #20 lists Hattori and compare-to-threshold as in-scope baselines that had never been fit at scale. All three are now fit on the **same** fixed 149-mouse held-out cohort, and **Bari is not the strongest of them**. The comparison of record is now against the *best* classical model, and the margin is correspondingly smaller. The GRU still wins on every model and in every (variant, D) cell.

**Design (identical across all three models).** One differential-evolution fit per held-out mouse on that mouse's own train sessions, scored on its own eval sessions — no cross-mouse sharing, no embeddings, no population prior. Same fixed n=149 cohort, same eval sessions, same `snapshot=20260603` as the GRU (1.01M eval trials). Fitted with `skip_train_fit=false`, so each run *also* fitted the 614 training mice — those per-subject parameters are the `model2` artifact consumed by the embedding analyses (#24 / #27).

Per-curriculum breakdown (Bari, n=149, unchanged): Uncoupled Baiting 71 (0.725), None 32 (0.683), Mixed 27 (0.733), Uncoupled Without Baiting 17 (0.773), Coupled Baiting 2 (0.613) — classical RL underperforms most on `None` (early-stage, less structured behavior).

**Headline (v2, D=614).** Against the *strongest* classical model (Compare-to-threshold) the GRU's margin is **+0.01129** (100% of mice, Wilcoxon p=1.0e-25). The originally-reported margin was measured against Bari (L1F1_CK1) (**+0.01352**), which is **not** the strongest classical model — so the earlier figure overstated the gap by **0.00223** (20%). The GRU still wins, on every model and every cell.

> **Note on the reference band.** Results 1/4/5/7 draw their RL band from **Bari (L1F1_CK1)** (`band_model` in `rl_baseline.json`), the model those figures were originally written against. It is not the strongest baseline; treat those bands as a *historical* reference and this table as the comparison of record.

- **GRU beats per-mouse RL at every (variant, D) cell, including D=10.** A population GRU trained on as few as 10 other mice predicts a new mouse better than fitting *that same mouse's own data* with a classical Q-learner (97% of mice, p~1e-25). This is strong evidence the GRU exploits cross-mouse structure that per-mouse RL cannot.
- **Large-D gain over RL is the dominant signal in this study.** v2 D=614 vs RL: +0.0136 per mouse (100%, p=3e-26) — **~9× the v2−v1 SC effect (+0.0015)** and **~2× the within-GRU D=10→614 gain (+0.0063)**. The population-vs-per-mouse cognitive-model gap dwarfs both session conditioning and within-GRU data scaling.
- **SC's incremental win over RL is consistent with Result 1.** v2 D=614 beats RL by +0.0136; v1 D=614 by +0.0121 → v2 adds +0.00146 over v1, matching the matched-pair v2−v1 SC effect.

**Caveats** (also in `rl_baseline_verdict.md`): (a) the RL baseline has **no D-axis** — it is a per-mouse independent fit, not a population model; this tests "GRU vs classical RL on the same data" (yes), not "does more mice help a population RL?" (deferred hierarchical-Bayesian baseline; see `FUTURE_DIRECTIONS.md`); (b) **L1F1_CK1** is the simplest agent in this family — richer agents (more learn/forget rates; `ForagerLossCounting`; `ForagerCompareThreshold`) may close part of the gap; (c) single DE optimizer seed (DE is stable; add seeds [1, 2] for a tighter check).
